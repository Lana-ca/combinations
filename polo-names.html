The Travels of Marco Polo & the Vocabulary of Power

Marco Polo did not merely observe the world — he named it, and in naming it, described it. This analysis maps the adjectives he attaches to persons, rulers, and places throughout his travels, asking what patterns emerge when language becomes the instrument of empire.

A note on "great"

Marco's most-deployed adjective functions less as description than as a stamp of legibility — an assurance to his European audience that whatever he is describing can be ranked and accommodated within their existing framework of wonder. What a name does not receive is equally telling: places Marco marks as desolate, wild, or cold receive no "great" — they exist outside the economy of admiration.

Moral asymmetry: persons vs. places

Vice and corruption in Marco's vocabulary attach almost exclusively to persons, never to landscapes. Places are barren, desolate, or wild — conditions that invite civilizing intervention — but only people are treacherous, wicked, or false. This asymmetry is not neutral: moral failure is personalized and thus punishable, while geographic difficulty is naturalized and thus conquerable. The distinction maps precisely onto who receives military attention in the text.

Christian rulers and the virtue cluster

Names Marco identifies as Christian — Prester John, Abaga, Argon, Bayan, Nayan (even in rebellion) — cluster disproportionately in the Virtue & Morality column. Non-Christian administrators, most pointedly Ahmad, are assigned the vice cluster in near totality. Whether or not this reflects observed behavior, it reflects a linguistic system in which religious identity precedes and conditions description. The name comes first; the adjective follows from what the name already means.
